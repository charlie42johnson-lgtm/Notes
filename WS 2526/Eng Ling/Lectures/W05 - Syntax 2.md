# Phases:
- at the kind of intermediate level between words and clauses
- less complex than clauses
- bigger groups of words around one word class
- defined by word class that informs them

### Many word classes can expand/project into phrases:

| Word Class:        | Phrase Type:              | Examples:                                                                     |
| ------------------ | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| N (noun)           | NP (noun phrase)          | ***book***, a ***book***, all my last books, the ***books*** I lost yesterday |
| V (verb)           | VP (verb phrase           | ***buy***, have ***bought***, ***bought*** two books on history               |
| Adj (adjective)    | AdjP (adjective phrase)   | ***cold***, incredibly ***cold***, just a tiny bit ***cold***                 |
| Adv (adverb        | AdvP (adverb phrase)      | ***recently***, very ***recently***                                           |
| Prep (preposition) | PP (prepositional phrase) | ***at*** the airport, ***in*** the bookstore                                  |

## Structure of Phrases:
### Head: (bold elements in examples above)
- obligatory element of phrase 
- determines the kind of phrase (NP, VP…)
### Dependents:
- other elements of phrases (=> pre- and -post modifying elements)

## Endocentric phrases:
- most phrases are these
- can often be replaced entirely by their head in a sentence

# Clauses: Structural types:

Definition: a constituent with one subject verb structure, it may stand on its own (constituent a sentence by itself)

## Structural types of clauses:
•	zooms in on verb that is part of clauses and the specific shape and form of the verb

### Finite clause:
•	the question of whether the verb is inflected or not, if it is inflected —> it is finite
•	e.g. 
	***Mary*** ***Stays***
    ***Mary*** ***stays*** and ***I’ll stay too*.****

### Non-finite clause:
•	the verb does not carry inflection (but not always; -ed, -ing, …)
•	e.g.
	Someone wants ***Mary*** ***to*** ***stay***.
    ***While*** ***leaving***, I waved goodbye.
    ***Left*** ***by*** ***Mary***, I was sad.

### Verbless clauses:
•	no verb in the clause
•	e.g. Although always helpful


---

# Finite clauses:
a clause containing a finite verb
•	simple sentences, that-clauses / wh-clauses
•	She ***attends*** the classes regularly.

Finite verb:
•	1st or only verb in a VP
•	concord of number and person between subject and verb
•	inflected for verbal categories of person, number, tense
•	e.g. 
	she ***writes***
    she ***lived*** in Paris
 

---

# Non-finite clauses:

•	can’t stand as a sentence on their own
•	always sub-clauses, always need to appear in a more complex clause
•	non-finite clauses might be marked for passive or perfect

a clause whose verb elements are all non-finite
•	no subject — verb agreement / concord
•	no tense-marking, no modal auxiliaries
•	possible forms: -ing / -ed participle, to-inf., gerund, bare inf. (term used to refer to the nonfinite form of a verb)

•	a sub-clause that does not have a verb elements (usually the copula be is omitted)
•	does’nt have a verb elements due to elypse => verb element that could easily be there but is left out for certain reasons
•	e.g. ***Although*** ***always*** ***helpful***, she was not much liked. —> Although <span style="color:rgb(192, 0, 0)">she</span> <span style="color:rgb(192, 0, 0)">was</span> always helpful, she was not much liked.

# Sentences and sentence types:

## Simple, complex and compound sentences:

### Simple sentence:
One independent clause:
•	one single gramm. unit with one verbal core
Peter was hungry.

### Compound sentence:
Two or more independent clauses:
•	both parts can stand on their own
•	created by coordination conjunctions; and, or, but, for, so, …
Peter was hungry and he bought a sandwich.

For coordination conjunctions we have a limited set: the FANBOYS set:

| F   | A   | N   | B   | O   | Y   | S   |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| for | and | nor | but | or  | yet | so  |

# Relative clauses: Hinrichs et al. (2015):

Aim and research question: The effect of prescriptivism on (standard) language use:
Do prescriptive language guides have an effect on how people use language? If yes, how can we show this? 

## Restrictive and non-restrictive relative clauses:

### Restrictive relative clauses:
•	not separated by comma
•	clause identifies the intended referent:
There are several sandwiches in the fridge. Jenifer takes the sandwich **which is closest to her.**
==> restrictive clause specifically identifies “that” sandwich

•	Restrictive relative clauses with non-human referents can be introduced with which, that or zero relativiser:
      —> the sandwich that is closest to her = prescriptive style guides recommend that —> clear differentiation between restrictive and non-restrictive
      —> the sandwich which is closest to her
      —> the sandwich closest to her

### Non-restrictive relative clauses:
•	separated by comma
•	clause provides additional info. on a referent that was already known before:
Jennifer takes Jack’s sandwich, which has cheese in it.
==> the referent is already identified

•	Non-restrictive relative clauses with non-human referents can only be introduced with which relativiser:
      —> *Jack’s sandwich, that has cheese in it
      —> *Jack’s sandwich, (has) cheese in it

## Results of study:

Increase of that in restrictive relative clauses between 1960s and 1990s
•	decrease of which
•	zero option remains more or less uncharged 

Some evidence for a casual relation between style guide recommendations and change
•	texts that adhere to style guide recommendations overall (espec. with respect to preposition stranding) are more likely to use that instead of which
