# Present Perfect: Simple or Progressive?

## Overview

The choice between **Present Perfect Simple** and **Present Perfect Progressive** depends on:
1. Whether the verb is **stative** or **dynamic**
2. Whether **completion** of the situation is relevant

---

## PRESENT PERFECT SIMPLE

### When to Use Simple

Simple is used if:
- The meaning of the verb is **stative**, OR
- The meaning of the verb is **dynamic**, AND completion of the situation is relevant

---

### Case 1: Stative Verbs

#### Rule
**Stative verbs** always use **simple aspect** (in any tense, including perfect).

**Example:**
- "**Have you been busy?**" ~ "**Have you been** well?"
  - **been** = stative use of be
  - Simple aspect required
  - No progressive form: *"Have you been being busy?"* ❌

**Common Stative Categories:**
- Mental states: know, believe, think (opinion), understand
- Emotional states: love, hate, like, want
- Possession: have, own, possess
- States of being: be, seem, appear
- Perception: see, hear, smell, taste (stative sense)

---

### Case 2: Dynamic Verbs - Completion is Relevant

When completion of the situation is relevant, use **simple aspect**.

There are several sub-cases:

#### a) Situation Happens At Least Once (At Any Time Before Now)

**What is relevant:** That a situation happens **at least once** at any time before now.

**Example:**
- "I**'ve even worn** these shoes in heavy rain, but they seem to be indestructible."
  - **Relevant:** The fact that wearing happened at least once
  - **Not relevant:** Whether the wearing is complete
  - **Simple required**

**More Examples:**
- I **'ve visited** Paris several times.
- She **has read** that book before.
- They **have eaten** at that restaurant.

**Key Point:** Focus is on **existence of occurrence(s)**, not on completion.

---

#### b) How Many Times (Before Now)

**What is relevant:** **How many times** a situation happens before now.

**Example:**
- "I**'ve always used** the OED."
  - **always + perfect** = normal meaning of "every time"
  - Focus: frequency/count
  - **Simple required**

**Important Note:**
- **always + perfect** has the normal meaning of "every time"
- This is different from **always + progressive** (see below)

**More Examples:**
- I **'ve read** that book three times.
- She **has called** him twice today.
- They **have been** to London many times.

---

#### c) How Much / How Many is Completed Now

**What is relevant:** How much / how many **is completed** now.

**Example:**
- "I**'ve written** no fewer than 5 letters this morning."
  - **Relevant:** The completion of 5 letters
  - **Focus:** Quantified result achieved
  - **Simple required**

**More Examples:**
- I **'ve read** three chapters of this book.
- She **has baked** two cakes.
- They **have completed** half the project.

**Key Point:** The **quantity completed** is what matters.

---

#### d) Recent Situations - Effect of Completion

**What is relevant:** The **effect of completion**.

**Examples:**

**Example 1:**
- "Good for you — you**'ve mown** the lawn, I see. We can have tea out here this afternoon."
  - **Relevant:** The lawn is now mown (result of completion)
  - **Effect:** Can have tea outside
  - **Simple required**

**Example 2:**
- "The house is fit to be seen at last. I**'ve cleaned** it from top to bottom."
  - **Relevant:** The house is now clean (result)
  - **Effect:** Fit to be seen
  - **Simple required**

**Key Point:** The **present result** of the completed action is what matters.

---

## PRESENT PERFECT PROGRESSIVE

### When to Use Progressive

Progressive is used if:
- The meaning of the verb is **dynamic**, AND
- **Completion is not relevant**

**Form:** have/has been + -ing

---

### Case 1: Activity Throughout a Period (Continues into Present)

**What is relevant:** Activity throughout a specified period that **continues into the present**.

**Completion:** Not important (may or may not be finished).

**Examples:**

**Example 1:**
- "I**'ve been wearing** these shoes for 10 years."
  - **Relevant:** Ongoing activity over 10-year period
  - **Not relevant:** Whether the wearing is "complete"
  - **Progressive required**

**Example 2:**
- "I**'ve been using** the OED all my life, but I still find myself misinterpreting what it says."
  - **Relevant:** Lifelong activity
  - **Not relevant:** Whether usage is complete
  - **Progressive required**

**Example 3:**
- "He **had been using** the OED for over 20 years, but he still found himself misinterpreting it."
  - **Past perfect progressive** (parallel structure)
  - Activity up to past reference point
  - **Progressive required**

**Example 4:**
- "I**'ve been writing** letters all morning."
  - **Relevant:** How the morning has been spent
  - **Not relevant:** Whether letters are finished
  - **Progressive required**

**Key Point:** Focus on **ongoing activity**, not completion.

---

### Case 2: Recent Situations - How Time Was Spent

**What is relevant:** How time up to now or just before now has been spent.

**Characteristics:**
- Situation may or may not be finished now
- Any relevant effect is **not** the effect of completion
- Focus is on the **activity/process** itself

**Examples:**

**Example 1:**
- "I**'ve been mowing** the lawn and I**'m tired out**."
  - **Relevant:** Tiredness from the activity
  - **Not relevant:** Whether lawn is completely mown
  - **Effect is from activity, not completion**
  - **Progressive required**

**Contrast with Simple:**
- "I**'ve mown** the lawn and we can have tea outside."
  - Effect is from completion (lawn is mown)
  - Simple required

**Example 2:**
- "My hands are in a dreadful state. I**'ve been cleaning** my bike."
  - **Relevant:** Hands dirty from cleaning activity
  - **Not relevant:** Whether bike is completely clean
  - **Effect is from activity, not completion**
  - **Progressive required**

**Key Distinction:**

| Progressive | Simple |
|-------------|--------|
| Effect from **activity/process** | Effect from **completion** |
| Hands dirty from cleaning | Bike is now clean |
| Tired from mowing | Lawn is now mown |

---

## SUMMARY TABLE: SIMPLE vs. PROGRESSIVE

### Present Perfect Simple

| When Used | What is Relevant | Examples |
|-----------|------------------|----------|
| **Stative verbs** | The state itself | Have you been well? |
| **At least once** | Existence of occurrence | I've worn these shoes in rain. |
| **Frequency** | How many times | I've always used the OED. |
| **Quantity** | How much completed | I've written 5 letters. |
| **Recent completion** | Effect of completion | I've mown the lawn. (now mown) |

### Present Perfect Progressive

| When Used | What is Relevant | Examples |
|-----------|------------------|----------|
| **Ongoing activity** | Activity throughout period | I've been wearing these shoes for 10 years. |
| **Recent activity** | How time was spent | I've been mowing the lawn. (tired now) |
| **Activity effects** | Effects from process, not completion | I've been cleaning my bike. (hands dirty) |

---

## DECISION FLOWCHART

```
Is the verb stative?
│
├─ YES → Use SIMPLE
│        (Have you been busy?)
│
└─ NO → Is completion relevant?
         │
         ├─ YES → Use SIMPLE
         │        • At least once
         │        • How many times
         │        • Quantity completed
         │        • Effect of completion
         │
         └─ NO → Use PROGRESSIVE
                  • Ongoing activity
                  • How time was spent
                  • Activity effects
```

## DETAILED EXAMPLES WITH ANALYSIS

### Example 1: At Least Once (Simple)

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve even worn** these shoes in heavy rain, but they seem to be indestructible."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (wear) |
| **Focus** | That wearing occurred at least once |
| **Completion relevant?** | No - not about completion |
| **Why Simple?** | Focus is on existence of occurrence |
| **Result meaning** | I now know they're indestructible |

**Wrong:** *"I've even been wearing these shoes..."* (would imply ongoing activity, not relevant)

---

### Example 2: Ongoing Activity (Progressive)

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve been wearing** these shoes for 10 years."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (wear) |
| **Focus** | Activity throughout 10-year period |
| **Completion relevant?** | No - may continue |
| **Why Progressive?** | Focus on ongoing activity, not completion |
| **Time expression** | for 10 years (duration) |

**Wrong:** *"I've worn these shoes for 10 years."* (would imply completed series)

---

### Example 3: Always + Simple (Frequency)

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve always used** the OED."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (use) |
| **Focus** | Frequency (every time) |
| **Completion relevant?** | No - about regular occurrence |
| **Why Simple?** | always + perfect = normal frequency meaning |
| **Meaning** | Every time I needed a dictionary, I used OED |

**Compare Always + Progressive:**
- "He's always bringing me flowers." (sporadic, not every time)
- Different meaning - see "always + progressive" special use

---

### Example 4: Quantity Completed (Simple)

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve written** no fewer than 5 letters this morning."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (write) |
| **Focus** | Quantity completed (5 letters) |
| **Completion relevant?** | Yes - exactly 5 completed |
| **Why Simple?** | Focus on quantified result |
| **Time expression** | this morning (period up to now) |

**Wrong:** *"I've been writing no fewer than 5 letters."* (progressive doesn't count completions)

---

### Example 5: Effect of Completion (Simple)

**Sentence:**
- "Good for you — you**'ve mown** the lawn, I see. We can have tea out here this afternoon."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (mow) |
| **Focus** | Result: lawn is now mown |
| **Completion relevant?** | Yes - completion creates result |
| **Why Simple?** | Effect of completion matters |
| **Present result** | Can have tea outside |

---

### Example 6: Activity Effect (Progressive)

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve been mowing** the lawn and I**'m tired out**."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (mow) |
| **Focus** | Effect from activity (tiredness) |
| **Completion relevant?** | No - tired from effort, not completion |
| **Why Progressive?** | Effect from process, not result |
| **Present result** | I'm tired (from the work) |

**Different from Simple:**
- Simple: lawn is mown (result of completion)
- Progressive: I'm tired (result of activity)

---

### Example 7: All Morning - Progressive

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve been writing** letters all morning."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (write) |
| **Focus** | How morning has been spent |
| **Completion relevant?** | No - may write more |
| **Why Progressive?** | Activity throughout period |
| **Time expression** | all morning (duration) |

**Contrast:**
- "I've written letters all morning." (implies finished writing)
- "I've been writing letters all morning." (activity ongoing)

---

### Example 8: All Morning - Simple

**Sentence:**
- "I**'ve written** no fewer than 5 letters this morning."

**Analysis:**
| Factor | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Verb type** | Dynamic (write) |
| **Focus** | Quantity achieved (5) |
| **Completion relevant?** | Yes - exactly 5 finished |
| **Why Simple?** | Counting completed items |
| **Achievement focus** | Look what I've accomplished! |

## SPECIAL NOTE: ALWAYS

### Always + Perfect Simple

**Meaning:** Every time (normal frequency meaning)

**Example:**
- "I**'ve always used** the OED."
  - Meaning: Every time I needed a dictionary, I used OED
  - Normal frequency meaning

### Always + Progressive

**Meaning:** Frequently but sporadically (characteristic behavior)

**Example:**
- "He**'s always bringing** me flowers."
  - Meaning: He frequently brings flowers (not every time)
  - Characteristic behavior
  - Unpredictable timing

**See also:** "Series and Aspect" handout for more on always + progressive

## COMMON MISTAKES

### Mistake 1: Using Progressive for Stative

**Wrong:** *I**'ve been knowing** him for years.*

**Correct:** I**'ve known** him for years.

**Why:** know = stative → simple only

---

### Mistake 2: Using Simple for Ongoing Activity

**Wrong:** *I**'ve worn** these shoes for 10 years.* (if still wearing)

**Better:** I**'ve been wearing** these shoes for 10 years.

**Why:** Focus on ongoing activity, not completed series

**Exception:** I**'ve worn** these shoes for 10 years, but I**'ve been wearing** my new ones lately.
  - First: completed series of wearing
  - Second: current ongoing activity

---

### Mistake 3: Using Progressive for Counted Completions

**Wrong:** *I**'ve been writing** 5 letters this morning.*

**Correct:** I**'ve written** 5 letters this morning.

**Why:** Counting completions → simple required

---

### Mistake 4: Using Simple for Activity Effects

**Wrong:** *I**'ve cleaned** my bike and my hands are dirty.* (implies hands should be clean)

**Better:** I**'ve been cleaning** my bike and my hands are dirty.

**Why:** Dirty hands = effect of activity (progressive)

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Stative verbs** → always simple
2. **Dynamic + completion relevant** → simple
3. **Dynamic + completion not relevant** → progressive
4. **Simple** focuses on: occurrence, frequency, quantity, completion effects
5. **Progressive** focuses on: ongoing activity, how time was spent, activity effects
6. **Always + simple** = every time (frequency)
7. **Always + progressive** = frequently but sporadically
8. **Ask:** Is completion what matters? If yes → simple. If no → progressive.
