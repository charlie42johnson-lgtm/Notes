## Lektion 1: Das altkirchenslavische Schriftsystem

### 1.1 Die Geschichte der Schriften (The History of Scripts)

**[[Kyrillische Schrift]] (Cyrillic Script)**:
- Created by Cyril and Methodius in 9th century
- Based on Greek uncial script
- Used for liturgical texts in Slavic languages
- Gradually replaced Glagolitic in most areas

**[[Glagolitische Schrift]] (Glagolitic Script)**:
- The first Slavic alphabet, created by Cyril
- More complex than Cyrillic
- Preserved longer in Croatia and Dalmatia
- Name comes from Old Church Slavonic "glagolĭ" (word, speech)

**[[Asbuka]] (Alphabet)**:
- The Cyrillic alphabet as used in Old Church Slavonic
- Contains letters derived from Greek with additions for Slavic sounds

### 1.5 Palatalization

#### [[Palatalization]] Definition

The process by which a consonant becomes palatal (pronounced with the middle of the tongue raised toward the hard palate) or palatalized.

#### Types of Palatalization in OCS

1. **[[Soft Palatalization]]**: Consonant becomes palatalized before front vowels
2. **[[Hard Palatalization]]**: Consonant remains non-palatalized

#### [[Soft Consonants]] (Palatalized)

Consonants that are palatalized before front vowels (/i/, /e/, /ě/, /ę/, /j/):
- /tʲ/, /dʲ/, /sʲ/, /zʲ/, /nʲ/, /lʲ/, /rʲ/

#### [[Hard Consonants]] (Non-palatalized)

Consonants that resist palatalization:
- /k/, /g/, /x/, /c/, /č/, /š/, /ž/

#### [[Vowel Inventory]]

|-------|---------|------|
| **Close** | i | | u |
| | | | y |
| **Mid** | e | | o |
| | ě | | ǫ |
| | ę | | |

#### [[Nasal Vowels]]

OCS has two nasal vowels:
- **[[Big Yus]] (Ѫ ѫ)**: /õ/ - back nasal vowel
- **[[Little Yus]] (Ѧ ѧ)**: /ẽ/ - front nasal vowel

#### [[Reduced Vowels]] (Jers)

Two reduced (ultra-short) vowels:
- **[[Front Jer]] (Ь ь)**: /ĭ/ - ultra-short front vowel
- **[[Back Jer]] (Ъ ъ)**: /ŭ/ - ultra-short back vowel

**Note**: Jers were lost in later stages of Slavic languages ("fall of the jers").

### 1.7 Phonological Processes

#### [[Jotation]]

The process where /j/ (jot) combines with following vowel or consonant:
- /j/ + /a/ → /ja/ (written я)
- /j/ + /e/ → /jɛ/ (written є)
- /j/ + /i/ → /ji/ (written ї)
- /j/ + /u/ → /ju/ (written ю)

#### [[Vowel Reduction]]

In unstressed syllables, non-high vowels often reduce to jers or schwa.

#### [[Consonant Assimilation]]

Voicing assimilation: voiced consonants become voiceless before voiceless consonants.

### 1.8 Orthographic Conventions

#### Writing Conventions

- **Scriptio continua**: Words often written together without spaces
- **Breathing marks**: Used in early manuscripts (similar to Greek)
- **Accent marks**: Rare in early manuscripts, used later

#### Abbreviations

Common abbreviations in manuscripts:
- **Nomina sacra**: Sacred names abbreviated
- **Suspensions**: Final letters omitted
- **Contractions**: Letters omitted within word

## Lektion 2: Die Nominalflexion: o-Stämme

### 2.1 Nominal Inflection Overview

**[[Noun]] (Substantiv)**: A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.

**[[Adjective]] (Adjektiv)**: A word that modifies or describes a noun.

**[[Pronoun]] (Pronomen)**: A word that takes the place of a noun.

### 2.2 Grammatical Categories

#### [[Case]] (Kasus, Fall)

OCS has seven cases:

1. **[[Nominative]] (Nominativ)**: Subject case; marks the doer of the action
   - Example: člověkъ "the man" (subject)

2. **[[Accusative]] (Akkusativ)**: Direct object case; marks the receiver of the action
   - Example: člověka "the man" (direct object)

3. **[[Genitive]] (Genitiv)**: Possessive case; marks possession, origin
   - Example: člověka "of the man", "the man's"

4. **[[Dative]] (Dativ)**: Indirect object case; marks the beneficiary
   - Example: člověku "to the man", "for the man"

5. **[[Instrumental]] (Instrumentalis)**: Means/instrument case; marks the tool or means
   - Example: člověkomь "by the man", "with the man" (as instrument)

6. **[[Locative]] (Lokativ)**: Location case; marks place or time
   - Example: člověcě "in the man", "on the man"

7. **[[Vocative]] (Vokativ)**: Direct address case; used when calling someone
   - Example: člověče "O man!", "man!" (address)

#### [[Number]] (Numerus)

- **[[Singular]] (Singularis)**: one entity
- **[[Dual]] (Dualis)**: exactly two entities (special OCS feature)
- **[[Plural]] (Pluralis)**: more than two entities

#### [[Gender]] (Genus)

- **[[Masculine]] (Maskulinum)**: masculine gender
- **[[Feminine]] (Femininum)**: feminine gender  
- **[[Neuter]] (Neutrum)**: neuter gender

### 2.3 o-Stem Masculine Nouns

#### [[o-Stem]] Definition

Nouns whose stem ends in /o/ in the nominative singular.

#### Masculine o-Stem Paradigm: člověkъ "man"

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| **Nominative** | člověkъ | člověka | člověci |
| **Accusative** | člověka | člověka | člověky |
| **Genitive** | člověka | člověku | člověkŭ |
| **Dative** | člověku | člověkoma | člověkomŭ |
| **Instrumental** | člověkomь | člověkoma | člověky |
| **Locative** | člověcě | člověku | člověcěxŭ |
| **Vocative** | člověče | člověka | člověci |

#### Endings for Masculine o-Stems

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| **Nominative** | -ъ | -a | -i |
| **Accusative** | -a | -a | -y |
| **Genitive** | -a | -u | -ŭ |
| **Dative** | -u | -oma | -omŭ |
| **Instrumental** | -omь | -oma | -y |
| **Locative** | -ě | -u | -ěxŭ |
| **Vocative** | -e | -a | -i |

#### Notes on o-Stem Endings

- **Nominative singular -ъ**: The back jer (ъ) marks masculine nominative
- **Genitive plural -ŭ**: Shows the back jer (ъ) with acute accent
- **Vocative singular -e**: Palatalization of stem-final consonant
- **Instrumental plural -y**: Same as accusative plural

### 2.4 o-Stem Neuter Nouns

#### Neuter o-Stem Paradigm: mesto "place"

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| **Nominative** | mesto | mestě | mesta |
| **Accusative** | mesto | mestě | mesta |
| **Genitive** | mesta | mestu | mestŭ |
| **Dative** | mestu | mestoma | mestomŭ |
| **Instrumental** | mestomь | mestoma | mesty |
| **Locative** | mestě | mestu | mestěxŭ |
| **Vocative** | mesto | mestě | mesta |

#### Key Differences: Masculine vs. Neuter o-Stems

1. **Nominative/Accusative/Vocative**: Neuter uses -o (not -ъ)
2. **Nominative/Accusative/Vocative plural**: Neuter uses -a (not -i/-y)
3. **Instrumental plural**: Neuter uses -y (not -i)

### 2.5 o-Stem Adjectives (Hard)

| Case | Masc. Sg. | Neut. Sg. | Masc./Neut. Du. | Masc./Neut. Pl. |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Nom.** | novъ | novo | nova | novi |
| **Acc.** | nova | novo | nova | novy |
| **Gen.** | nova | nova | novu | novŭ |

- Adjectives agree with nouns in **case, number, and gender**
- Masculine and neuter adjectives share most endings
- The vocative singular ending -e causes palatalization

Words that modify nouns, agreeing in case, number, and gender.

## Lektion 6: Das Verb I: Konjugation

### 6.1 Verb Overview

#### [[Verb]] (Verb) Definition

A word that expresses action, occurrence, or state of being.

### 6.2 Grammatical Categories

#### [[Person]] (Persona)

1. **[[First Person]]**: I, we (azŭ, my)
2. **[[Second Person]]**: you (ty, vy)  
3. **[[Third Person]]**: he, she, it, they (onŭ, ona, ono, oni)

#### [[Number]] (Numerus)

- **[[Singular]]**: one subject
- **[[Dual]]**: two subjects
- **[[Plural]]**: more than two subjects

#### [[Tense]] (Tempus)

- **[[Present Tense]] (Präsens)**: actions happening now
- **[[Aorist]] (Aorist)**: simple past tense
- **[[Imperfect]] (Imperfekt)**: past continuous/habitual actions
- **[[Perfect]] (Perfekt)**: completed past actions (formed with l-participle + auxiliary)
- **[[Pluperfect]] (Plusquamperfekt)**: past before past
- **[[Future Tense]] (Futur)**: future actions

#### [[Mood]] (Modus)

- **[[Indicative]] (Indikativ)**: statements of fact
- **[[Imperative]] (Imperativ)**: commands
- **[[Conditional]] (Konditional)**: hypothetical situations

#### [[Voice]] (Genus verbi)

- **[[Active Voice]] (Aktiv)**: subject performs action
- **[[Passive Voice]] (Passiv)**: subject receives action

#### [[Aspect]] (Aspekt)

- **[[Perfective Aspect]] (Perfektiv)**: completed action, viewed as a whole
- **[[Imperfective Aspect]] (Imperfektiv)**: ongoing, habitual, or repeated action

## Lektion 7: Das Verb II: Aspekt und Tempus

### 7.1 Aspect System

#### [[Aspect]] (Aspekt) Definition

A grammatical category that expresses how an action relates to the flow of time - whether it's viewed as completed or ongoing.

#### [[Perfective Aspect]] (Perfektiv)

- Views action as a complete whole
- Focuses on beginning or end point
- Cannot be used for ongoing actions
- Often translated with simple past in English

**Examples**:
- nesti (perf.) "to carry (once, completely)"
- dati (perf.) "to give"

#### [[Imperfective Aspect]] (Imperfektiv)

- Views action as ongoing, habitual, or repeated
- Focuses on process rather than completion
- Can be used for continuous actions
- Often translated with progressive or habitual forms

**Examples**:
- nositi (imperf.) "to carry (repeatedly, habitually)"
- davati (imperf.) "to give (repeatedly)"

### 7.2 Aspect Pairs

Many verbs form aspect pairs:

| Imperfective | Perfective | Meaning |
|--------------|------------|---------|
| čĭtati | pročĭtati | "to read" |
| pisati | napisati | "to write" |
| delati | sdělati | "to do, make" |
| piti | vypiti | "to drink" |
| jěsti | pojěsti | "to eat" |
| viděti | uvĭděti | "to see" |

### 7.3 Formation of Perfective Verbs

#### Prefixation

Most common method: add prefix to imperfective verb

**Prefixes**:
- **do-**: completion (dělati → sdělati "to finish doing")
- **na-**: accumulation (pisati → napisati "to write (a lot)")
- **po-**: beginning (delati → podělati "to start doing")
- **pro-**: through (čĭtati → pročĭtati "to read through")
- **u-**: away (biti → ubiti "to kill")
- **vŭ-**: in (jěsti → vŭjěsti "to eat into")

#### Suffixation

Some perfective verbs are formed with different suffixes:
- goniti (imperf.) "to chase" → gnati (perf.) "to drive"

#### Suppletion

Some verbs have completely different stems:
- imati (imperf.) "to take" → jęti (perf.) "to take"

### 7.4 Tense System

#### [[Present Tense]] (Präsens)

- **Imperfective verbs**: present meaning ("I am reading")
- **Perfective verbs**: future meaning ("I will read (completely)")

#### [[Aorist]] (Aorist)

- Simple past tense
- Views action as single completed event
- Formed from aorist stem

**Formation**: aorist stem + personal endings

Example (nesti):
- 1st Sg: nesoхъ
- 2nd Sg: nese
- 3rd Sg: nese
- 1st Pl: nesoxomŭ
- 2nd Pl: nesoste
- 3rd Pl: nesošę

#### [[Imperfect]] (Imperfekt)

- Past continuous/habitual tense
- Views action as ongoing or repeated in the past
- Formed from imperfect stem

**Formation**: imperfect stem + -axŭ/-aše + personal endings

Example (nesti):
- 1st Sg: nesaxŭ
- 2nd Sg: nesaše
- 3rd Sg: nesaše
- 1st Pl: nesaxomŭ
- 2nd Pl: nesašete
- 3rd Pl: nesašę

#### [[Perfect]] (Perfekt)

- Compound tense: l-participle + present of "to be"
- Views action as completed with relevance to present

**Formation**: past active participle + auxiliary "byti"

Example (nesti):
- 1st Sg: neslŭ jesmĭ "I have carried"
- 2nd Sg: neslŭ jesi "you have carried"
- 3rd Sg: neslŭ jestŭ "he has carried"

#### [[Pluperfect]] (Plusquamperfekt)

- Compound tense: l-participle + imperfect of "to be"
- Views action as completed before another past action

**Formation**: past active participle + imperfect "byti"

Example (nesti):
- 1st Sg: neslŭ běaxŭ "I had carried"
- 2nd Sg: neslŭ běaše "you had carried"

#### [[Future Tense]] (Futur)

Three ways to express future:

1. **Perfective present**: Perfective verb in present tense
   - Example: pročĭtajǫ "I will read (completely)"

2. **Future with auxiliary**: budǫ + infinitive
   - Example: budǫ čĭtati "I will be reading"

3. **Future perfect**: budǫ + l-participle
   - Example: budǫ pročĭtalŭ "I will have read"

### 7.5 Conditional

#### [[Conditional]] Formation

L-participle + conditional auxiliary (byxŭ, by, by, etc.)

**Conditional of "byti"**:
- 1st Sg: byxŭ "I would be"
- 2nd Sg: by "you would be"
- 3rd Sg: by "he would be"

**Example with verb**:
- neslŭ byxŭ "I would carry"
- pročĭtalŭ by "he would read"

## Lektion 8: Das Verb III: Aktionsarten und Infinitiv

### 8.1 Aktionsarten (Action Types)

#### [[Aktionsart]] Definition

German term for "type of action" - lexical aspect that describes the internal temporal structure of an action.

#### Types of Aktionsarten

##### 1. [[Punctual]] (Punktuell)
- Actions viewed as instantaneous points
- Example: kriknǫti "to shout (once)"

##### 2. [[Durative]] (Durativ)
- Actions viewed as having duration
- Example: molčati "to be silent"

##### 3. [[Inchoative]] (Inchoativ)
- Actions marking the beginning of a state
- Example: zaspati "to fall asleep"

##### 4. [[Resultative]] (Resultativ)
- Actions emphasizing the result
- Example: napisati "to write (and finish)"

##### 5. [[Iterative]] (Iterativ)
- Repeated or habitual actions
- Example: čĭtati "to read (habitually)"

##### 6. [[Semelfactive]] (Semelfaktiv)
- Single, non-repeated actions
- Example: kriknǫti "to give a shout"

### 8.2 Verbal Prefixes and Aktionsarten

#### [[Prefixation]] and Meaning Changes

| Prefix | Primary Meaning | Example | Result |
|--------|----------------|---------|--------|
| **do-** | completion | dělati → sdělati | "to finish doing" |
| **na-** | accumulation | pisati → napisati | "to write (a lot)" |
| **po-** | beginning | delati → podělati | "to start doing" |
| **pro-** | through | čĭtati → pročĭtati | "to read through" |
| **pri-** | approach | idti → prijdti | "to arrive" |
| **u-** | away | biti → ubiti | "to kill" |
| **vy-** | out | jěsti → vyjěsti | "to eat up" |
| **za-** | behind, beginning | plakati → zaplakati | "to start crying" |
| **pere-** | across, re- | pisati → perespisati | "to rewrite" |
| **pod-** | under | jěsti → podjěsti | "to eat secretly" |
| **nad-** | over | jěsti → najěsti | "to overeat" |
| **ob-** | around | jěsti → objěsti | "to eat around" |
| **ot-** | away from | jěsti → otjěsti | "to eat away" |
| **do-** | to, until | jěsti → dojěsti | "to eat to the end" |
| **raz-** | apart | dělati → razdělati | "to divide" |
| **s-/so-** | together | dělati → sdělati | "to do together" |
| **iz-** | out of | jěsti → izjěsti | "to eat out of" |
| **vz-/voz-** | up | jěsti → vzjěsti | "to eat up" |
| **pri-** | to, at | jěsti → prijěsti | "to eat at" |

### 8.3 Secondary Imperfectives

#### [[Secondary Imperfective]] Formation

Some perfective verbs form new imperfective pairs:

| Perfective | Secondary Imperfective | Meaning |
|------------|------------------------|---------|
| pročĭtati | pročĭtyvati | "to read through (repeatedly)" |
| napisati | pisyvati | "to write (repeatedly)" |
| ubiti | ubivati | "to kill (repeatedly)" |

### 8.4 Infinitive and Supine

#### [[Infinitive]] (Infinitiv) Detailed

**Formation**: Infinitive stem + -ti

**Infinitive stems by conjugation**:
- 1st Conj: nes-ti (from present stem nes-)
- 2nd Conj: zna-ti (from present stem znaj-)
- 3rd Conj: xvali-ti (from present stem xvalĭ-)
- 4th Conj: děla-ti (from present stem dělaj-)
- 5th Conj: vidě-ti (from present stem vižd-)

#### [[Supine]] (Supinum) Detailed

**Formation**: Infinitive stem + -tŭ

**Use**: After verbs of motion to express purpose

**Examples**:
- idǫ čĭtatŭ "I go to read"
- pride vidětŭ "he came to see"

**Difference from Infinitive**:
- Infinitive: -ti ending, general use
- Supine: -tŭ ending, only after motion verbs

### 8.5 Verbal Nouns

#### [[Verbal Noun]] Formation

**-ĭnje suffix**: Forms abstract nouns from verbs

**Examples**:
- dělati "to do" → dělĭnje "doing, deed"
- xvaliti "to praise" → xvalĭnje "praise, praising"

### 8.6 Gerunds

#### [[Gerund]] (Verbal Adverb)

**Present Gerund**: present stem + -ęći
- Example: nesęći "while carrying"

**Past Gerund**: past stem + -vŭši
- Example: nesŭvŭši "having carried"

## Lektion 9: Syntax I: Der einfache Satz

### 9.1 Sentence Structure Overview

#### [[Simple Sentence]] (Einfacher Satz)

A sentence containing one independent clause.

#### [[Word Order]] (Wortstellung)

OCS has relatively free word order due to rich case system, but shows tendencies:

**Unmarked (neutral) word order**: Subject - Verb - Object (SVO)

**Marked word order**: For emphasis, focus, or stylistic variation

### 9.2 Constituent Order

#### [[Verb-Second]] (V2) Position

In main clauses, the finite verb often appears in second position:
- Člověkъ viditŭ děvǫ. "The man sees the girl."
- Děvǫ viditŭ člověkъ. "The girl, the man sees." (object fronted)

#### [[Verb-First]] Position

In questions, commands, and some narrative contexts:
- Viditŭ li člověkъ děvǫ? "Does the man see the girl?"
- Nesĭ knigǫ! "Carry the book!"

### 9.3 Case Usage in Simple Sentences

#### [[Nominative Case]]

**Primary function**: Marks the subject of a finite verb

**Examples**:
- Člověkъ čĭtetŭ. "The man is reading."
- Děvǫ vidimŭ. "We see the girl."

#### [[Accusative Case]]

**Primary function**: Marks the direct object of transitive verbs

**Examples**:
- Vidimŭ člověka. "We see the man."
- Knigǫ čĭtetŭ. "He is reading the book."

**With prepositions**: 
- vŭ "into, in" + accusative (direction)
- na "onto, on" + accusative (direction)
- za "behind, after" + accusative
- sŭ "with" + accusative (instrumental meaning)

#### [[Genitive Case]]

**Primary functions**:
1. **Possession**: "of, 's"
   - Kniga člověka. "The man's book."
2. **Partitive**: "some, any"
   - Dajǫ vody. "I give some water."
3. **After certain verbs**: 
   - trebovati + genitive "to need"
   - bojati sę + genitive "to fear"
4. **With negation**: object becomes genitive
   - Ne vidimŭ člověka. "I don't see the man."

#### [[Dative Case]]

**Primary functions**:
1. **Indirect object**: "to, for"
   - Dajǫ knigǫ člověku. "I give the book to the man."
2. **With certain verbs**:
   - pomagati + dative "to help"
   - verovati + dative "to believe"
3. **Possessive dative**: indicates possession
   - Jestŭ mi kniga. "I have a book." (literally: "To me is a book.")

#### [[Instrumental Case]]

**Primary functions**:
1. **Means/instrument**: "with, by means of"
   - Pišetŭ peromĭ. "He writes with a pen."
2. **Agent in passive**: "by"
   - Kniga pišetŭsę člověkomĭ. "The book is written by the man."
3. **With certain prepositions**:
   - sŭ "with" + instrumental
   - meždu "between" + instrumental
   - nadŭ "above" + instrumental
   - podŭ "under" + instrumental
   - predŭ "before" + instrumental

#### [[Locative Case]]

**Primary function**: Location (always with prepositions)

**Prepositions requiring locative**:
- vŭ "in" + locative (location)
- na "on" + locative (location)
- pri "at, near" + locative
- o "about, concerning" + locative

**Examples**:
- Vŭ domu. "In the house."
- Na stole. "On the table."
- O člověcě. "About the man."

#### [[Vocative Case]]

**Primary function**: Direct address

**Examples**:
- Člověče! "O man!"
- Brate! "O brother!"

### 9.4 Copular Constructions

#### [[Copula]] (Kopula): Byti "to be"

**Equational sentences**: Subject = Complement

**Examples**:
- Člověkъ jestŭ učitelĭ. "The man is a teacher."
- Ona jestŭ učitelĭnica. "She is a teacher (f.)."

**Predicate adjectives**:
- Člověkъ jestŭ dobry. "The man is good."
- Děvǫ jestŭ krasivaja. "The girl is beautiful."

**Predicate nominative**: Complement in nominative
- On jestŭ učitelĭ. "He is a teacher."

### 9.5 Existential Constructions

#### [[Existential]]: "There is/are"

**With byti + nominative**:
- Jestŭ člověkъ. "There is a man."
- Sǫtŭ ljudĭje. "There are people."

**With byti + genitive** (partitive meaning):
- Jestŭ vody. "There is some water."

### 9.6 Possessive Constructions

#### [[Possessive]]: "have"

OCS uses dative + byti construction:

**Dative possessor + nominative possessed**:
- Jestŭ mi kniga. "I have a book." (literally: "To me is a book.")
- Sǫtŭ namŭ děti. "We have children."

**Negative possession**:
- Nĭ jestŭ mi knigy. "I don't have a book." (genitive)

### 9.7 Impersonal Constructions

#### [[Impersonal]] Verbs

Verbs that have no explicit subject:

**Weather verbs**:
- Sĭnje. "It is snowing."
- Dĭždjĭtŭ. "It is raining."

**Modal-like constructions**:
- Nuzno jestŭ. "It is necessary."
- Možno jestŭ. "It is possible."

### 9.8 Questions

#### [[Yes/No Questions]]

**With interrogative particle li**:
- Čĭtajetŭ li onŭ knigǫ? "Is he reading the book?"
- Jestŭ li člověkъ? "Is there a man?"

**Verb-first without li** (less common):
- Čĭtajetŭ člověkъ knigǫ? "Does the man read the book?"

#### [[Wh-Questions]]

Interrogative pronouns and adverbs:

| Question Word | Meaning |
|---------------|---------|
| kŭto | who |
| čŭto | what |
| kakyjĭ | what kind of |
| kolikŭ | how much, how many |
| kŭde | where (to) |
| otkŭde | from where |
| kogda | when |
| kako | how |
| čĭmĭ | by what means |

**Examples**:
- Kŭto čĭtajetŭ? "Who is reading?"
- Čŭto viditŭ? "What does he see?"
- Kakyjĭ člověkъ? "What kind of man?"

### 9.9 Negation

#### [[Negation]] with ne

**Simple negation**:
- Ne čĭtajǫ. "I am not reading."
- Ne jestŭ člověka. "There is no man."

**Double negation** (negative concord):
- Nikŭto ne čĭtajetŭ. "No one is reading."
- Ničĭto ne vidimŭ. "I see nothing."

**Negative pronouns**:
- nikŭto "no one"
- ničĭto "nothing"
- nikakyjĭ "no kind of"
- nikolikŭ "no amount of"

## Lektion 10: Syntax II: Der zusammengesetzte Satz

### 10.1 Complex Sentences Overview

#### [[Complex Sentence]] (Zusammengesetzter Satz)

A sentence containing one or more dependent clauses in addition to the main clause.

### 10.2 Types of Dependent Clauses

#### [[Complement Clauses]] (Objektsätze)

Clauses that function as objects of verbs:

**With interrogative complementizers**:
- Vĭdomŭ, čĭto onŭ čĭtajetŭ. "I see that he is reading."
- Slyšimŭ, kako onŭ govori. "We hear how he speaks."

**Verbs of saying and thinking**:
- govori, čĭto... "he says that..."
- mysli, čĭto... "he thinks that..."

#### [[Relative Clauses]] (Relativsätze)

Clauses that modify nouns, introduced by relative pronouns:

**With iže**:
- Člověkъ, iže čĭtajetŭ knigǫ, jestŭ učenĭ. "The man who is reading the book is learned."

**Relative pronoun agreement**:
- iže (masc. sg.), iža (fem. sg.), iže (neut. sg.), iži (pl.)

**Word order in relative clauses**:
- Relative pronoun immediately follows antecedent
- Verb often in final position

#### [[Adverbial Clauses]]

Clauses that function as adverbials:

**Temporal clauses** (time):
- Kogda člověkъ čĭtajetŭ, onŭ uči sę. "When the man reads, he learns."

**Causal clauses** (reason):
- Poněže jestŭ učenĭ, onŭ uči družĭje. "Because he is learned, he teaches others."

**Conditional clauses** (condition):
- Ašče člověkъ čĭtajetŭ, onŭ uči sę. "If the man reads, he learns."

**Purpose clauses** (purpose):
- Člověkъ čĭtajetŭ, da uči sę. "The man reads so that he may learn."

**Concessive clauses** (concession):
- Išče jestŭ učenĭ, onŭ jošte uči sę. "Although he is learned, he still learns."

### 10.3 Conjunctions

#### [[Coordinating Conjunctions]] (nebenordnende Konjunktionen)

| Conjunction | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| i | and |
| a | and, but (contrasting) |
| ali | but, or |
| ili | or |
| nĭ | but |
| bo | for, because |

**Examples**:
- Člověkъ čĭtajetŭ i uči sę. "The man reads and learns."
- Ona jestŭ učitelĭnica, a on jestŭ učenĭkŭ. "She is a teacher, and/but he is a student."

#### [[Subordinating Conjunctions]] (unterordnende Konjunktionen)

| Conjunction | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| čĭto | that |
| iže | who, which |
| kogda | when |
| ašče | if |
| poněže | because |
| jako | as, that |
| da | that, so that |
| išče | although |
| dokolě | while, as long as |
| eda | lest, that not |

### 10.4 Word Order in Complex Sentences

#### [[Verb-Second Constraint]] in Main Clauses

When a subordinate clause precedes the main clause, the main clause verb comes first:

**Subordinate clause + main clause verb + subject**:
- Ašče člověkъ čĭtajetŭ, uči sę onŭ. "If the man reads, he learns."
- Kogda pride, vide onŭ člověka. "When he came, he saw the man."

#### [[Subordinate Clause Word Order]]

More flexible, but typically:
- Conjunction first
- Subject before verb (but not always)
- Verb-final tendency

### 10.5 Sequence of Tenses

#### [[Tense Agreement]] in Complex Sentences

**Main clause present + subordinate clause present**:
- Govorǫ, čĭto onŭ čĭtajetŭ. "I say that he is reading."

**Main clause past + subordinate clause past**:
- Govorixašete, čĭto onŭ čĭtajaše. "You were saying that he was reading."

**Main clause present + subordinate clause future**:
- Znajǫ, čĭto onŭ bǫdetŭ čĭtati. "I know that he will read."

### 10.6 Mood in Subordinate Clauses

#### [[Subjunctive-like]] Constructions

**With da**:
- Hočǫ, da čĭtajetŭ. "I want him to read." (literally: "I want that he read.")
- Trebǫ, da pride. "I need him to come."

**Sequence of moods**:
- Main clause verb of desire/wish/command + da + present tense

### 10.7 Relative Pronoun Usage

#### [[Relative Pronoun]] Agreement

**iže** must agree with its antecedent in gender and number, case determined by function in relative clause:

- Člověkъ, ižego vidimŭ... "The man whom we see..."
- Kniga, ižojo čĭtajemŭ... "The book with which we read..."

**Possessive relative**:
- Člověkъ, ižemu jestŭ kniga... "The man whose book it is..."

### 10.8 Comparative and Superlative Clauses

#### [[Comparative Clauses]]

With jako "as, than":
- Onŭ jestŭ bolĭši jako ja. "He is bigger than I."
- Čĭtajetŭ bolĭše jako my. "He reads more than we do."

#### [[Superlative Constructions]]

With vŭsĭxŭ "of all":
- Onŭ jestŭ najbolĭši vŭsĭxŭ. "He is the biggest of all."

### 10.9 Infinitive Clauses

#### [[Infinitive]] as Complement

Some verbs take infinitive complements:
- Možetŭ čĭtati. "He can read."
- Hočetŭ čĭtati. "He wants to read."
- Učitŭ sę čĭtati. "He learns to read."

#### [[Infinitive]] as Purpose

With verbs of motion:
- Idǫ čĭtati. "I go to read."
- Pride uči sę. "He came to learn."

## Lektion 11: Historische Phonologie I

### 11.1 Historical Phonology Overview

#### [[Historical Phonology]] Definition

The study of sound changes in a language over time.

### 11.2 Proto-Slavic to Old Church Slavonic

#### [[Proto-Slavic]] (Urslawisch)

The reconstructed ancestor of all Slavic languages.

#### [[Common Slavic]] (Gemeinslawisch)

The stage of Slavic before the major dialectal divisions.

### 11.3 Vowel System Changes

#### [[Proto-Slavic Vowels]]

Proto-Slavic had a reduced vowel system:

| Front | Central | Back |
|-------|---------|------|
| **High** | *i | *ĭ | *u | *ŭ |
| **Mid** | *e | *o |
| **Low** | *a | |
| **Nasal** | *ę | *ǫ |

#### [[Vowel Changes]]

##### 1. **[[Law of Open Syllables]]**
- Proto-Slavic eliminated closed syllables
- Consonant clusters were simplified or vowels inserted

##### 2. **[[Rise of Jers]]**
- Ultra-short vowels *ĭ and *ŭ developed from Proto-Indo-European short *i and *u

##### 3. **[[Nasal Vowels]]**
- *ę and *ǫ arose from Proto-Indo-European nasal consonant + vowel combinations

##### 4. **[[Jat']] (Ѣ)**
- Developed from Proto-Slavic *ě, which came from various Proto-Indo-European sources
- Different reflexes in different Slavic languages

### 11.4 Consonant Changes

#### [[First Palatalization]] (Progressive Palatalization)

*k, *g, *x → *č, *ž, *š before front vowels and *j

**Examples**:
- *korni → črĭnъ "black"
- *gosti → živǫ "I live"

#### [[Second Palatalization]] (Regressive Palatalization)

Before front vowels from *i, *ī, *e, *ē, *ę:

*k → *c
*g → *dz → *z  
*x → *s

**Examples**:
- *kъto → čĭto "what"
- *gъto → žĭto "wheat"

#### [[Third Palatalization]] (Iotation)

Before *j:

*t, *d, *s, *z, *n, *l, *r + *j → palatalized consonants

**Examples**:
- *tj → *ť
- *dj → *ď
- *sj → *š
- *zj → *ž

### 11.5 Sound Laws

#### [[Slavic Liquid Metathesis]]

In South and West Slavic:
- *or, *ol, *er, *el → *ra, *la, *re, *le

**Examples**:
- *orktis → rǫka "hand"
- *bolto → blato "mud"

#### [[Slavic First Palatalization]]

*k, *g, *x → *č, *ž, *š before front vowels

#### [[Slavic Second Palatalization]]

Before new *i, *ī, *e, *ē, *ę from *oi, *ai, *ei:

*k, *g, *x → *c, *dz, *s

### 11.6 Accentuation

#### [[Proto-Slavic Accent]]

Proto-Slavic had free, mobile accent with distinctive pitch (rising vs. falling).

#### [[Accent Changes]]

**Dybo's Law**: Accented *jers lost accent to following syllable

**Meillet's Law**: Falling accent on non-final syllables became rising

#### [[Old Church Slavonic Accentuation]]

OCS preserved Proto-Slavic accent system fairly well, though written accentuation is limited in manuscripts.

## Lektion 12: Historische Phonologie II

### 12.1 Morphophonological Processes

#### [[Sandhi]] (Sound Changes at Word Boundaries)

**Progressive assimilation**: Sound influences following sound
**Regressive assimilation**: Sound is influenced by following sound

#### [[Vowel Reduction]]

**Strong position**: Vowels in stressed syllables or open syllables
**Weak position**: Vowels in unstressed closed syllables

**Jers in strong vs. weak position**:
- Strong jers: preserved as full vowels
- Weak jers: lost ("fall of the jers")

### 12.2 Consonant Assimilation

#### [[Voicing Assimilation]]

**Progressive**: Voiceless consonant causes following voiced consonant to become voiceless
- Example: vĭs- + -dĭ → vĭzdĭ "all"

**Regressive**: Voiced consonant causes preceding voiceless consonant to become voiced
- Example: sŭ + -bĭrati → zbĭrati "to gather"

#### [[Place Assimilation]]

**Dental + dental**: Assimilation common
**Labial + dental**: No assimilation
**Velar + dental**: Assimilation possible

### 12.3 Dissimilation

#### [[Consonant Dissimilation]]

Process where similar consonants become less similar:

**Examples**:
- *stĭnŭ → sĭnŭ "sleep" (loss of *t)
- *gvĭzda → zvĭzda "star" (metathesis)

### 12.4 Metathesis

#### [[Metathesis]] (Sound Reordering)

**Liquid metathesis**: *or, *ol → *ra, *la (in South/West Slavic)
- *orktis → rǫka "hand"

**Other metatheses**:
- *gvĭzda → zvĭzda "star"

### 12.5 Prothesis

#### [[Prothesis]] (Sound Addition at Beginning)

**Vowel prothesis**: Addition of vowel before initial consonant clusters
- Common in South Slavic
- Example: *mĭne → *imĭne → imę "name"

### 12.6 Epenthesis

#### [[Epenthesis]] (Sound Insertion)

**Consonant epenthesis**: Insertion of consonant to break up difficult clusters
- Example: *sĭnŭ → sŭnŭ "sleep" (insertion of ъ)

**Vowel epenthesis**: Insertion of vowel
- Example: *čĭrnŭ → črĭnŭ "black" (loss of epenthetic vowel)

### 12.7 Syncope

#### [[Syncope]] (Vowel Loss)

Loss of unstressed vowels in certain positions:
- Example: *sŭnŭ → sŭnŭ "sleep" (vowel loss in weak position)

### 12.8 Haplology

#### [[Haplology]] (Repeated Syllable Loss)

Loss of one of two identical or similar syllables:
- Example: *nebese → nebe "sky" (in some forms)

## Lektion 13: Historische Phonologie III

### 13.1 Morphophonology

#### [[Morphophonology]] Definition

The study of how morphology (word formation) interacts with phonology (sound patterns).

### 13.2 Alternations

#### [[Morphophonological Alternations]]

**Vowel-zero alternations**: Presence vs. absence of vowel in different forms
- Example: kostĭ "bone" vs. kost- in other cases

**Consonant alternations**: Different consonants in related forms
- Example: rǫka "hand" vs. rǫc- in oblique cases

### 13.3 Ablaut

#### [[Ablaut]] (Vowel Gradation)

Systematic vowel alternations in Proto-Indo-European roots:

**Ablaut grades**:
- **e-grade**: *bher- "carry"
- **o-grade**: *bhor- "carried"
- **zero-grade**: *bhr- "carrying"
- **lengthened e-grade**: *bhēr- "I carry"
- **lengthened o-grade**: *bhōr- "I carried"

**Examples in OCS**:
- berǫ "I take" (e-grade)
- berašę "they take" (zero-grade)
- bornikŭ "fighter" (o-grade)

### 13.4 Accentual Morphophonology

#### [[Accent Alternations]]

**Mobile accent**: Accent moves between stem and ending in different forms

**Example** (rǫka "hand"):
- Nominative: rǫka (stem accent)
- Genitive: rǫky (ending accent)
- Accusative: rǫkǫ (ending accent)

#### [[Accent and Morphology]]

Different morphological categories have different accent patterns:
- **Nouns**: Various accent patterns (fixed stem, fixed ending, mobile)
- **Verbs**: Present tense typically has stem accent, past tenses vary

### 13.5 Jers and Morphophonology

#### [[Jer Behavior]]

**Strong position**: Jers in stressed syllables or open syllables → preserved
**Weak position**: Jers in unstressed closed syllables → lost

**Morphological consequences**:
- Case forms may show jer loss differently
- Paradigms may have alternations based on jer position

**Example** (pĭsĭmŭ "letter"):
- Nominative: pĭsĭmŭ (both jers strong)
- Genitive: pĭsĭma (both jers strong)
- But other forms may show jer loss

### 13.6 Palatalization and Morphophonology

#### [[Palatalization Alternations]]

**Morphophonological effects**:
- Different case forms may show palatalization
- Present vs. past tense stems may differ

**Example** (kĭnǫti "to throw"):
- Present: kĭnǫ (no palatalization)
- Past participle: kĭnjenŭ (palatalization)

### 13.7 Historical Development from Proto-Slavic

#### [[Changes from Proto-Slavic to OCS]]

**Conservative features**:
- Preserved dual number
- Preserved aorist and imperfect tenses
- Rich case system maintained

**Innovative features**:
- Some vowel changes (especially jers)
- Accent changes in some dialects
- Morphological leveling in some paradigms

#### [[Dialectal Variation]]

OCS manuscripts show some dialectal features:
- **Eastern (Bulgarian)**: More conservative
- **Western (Macedonian)**: Some innovations
- **Serbian recensions**: Mix of features

## Lektion 14: Texte I: Evangelien und Psalter

### 14.1 Biblical Texts in OCS

#### [[Gospel Texts]] (Evangelien)

The primary corpus of OCS consists of biblical translations:

**[[Literal Translation]] (Wort für Wort)**:
- Close following of Greek word order
- Greek idioms preserved

**[[Dynamic Equivalence]]**:
- Meaning-based translation

#### [[Greek Influence]]

**Syntactic calques**:
- Greek word order patterns
- Greek case usage patterns

**Lexical calques**:
- Compound words modeled on Greek
- Semantic extensions based on Greek

#### [[Translation Challenges]]

**Cultural concepts**:
- Greek philosophical terms
- Jewish cultural practices
- Christian theological concepts

**Linguistic challenges**:
- Complex Greek syntax
- Abstract vocabulary
- Figurative language

#### [[Liturgical Use]]

#### [[Liturgical Language]]

OCS became the **liturgical language** of Orthodox Slavs:
- Preserved in Church Slavonic
- Used in services for centuries
- Basis for modern liturgical languages

## Lektion 15: Texte II: Zusammenfassung

### 15.1 Summary of Old Church Slavonic

#### [[OCS as a Language]]

**Historical position**:
- First written Slavic language
- Literary language, not spoken vernacular
- Basis for Church Slavonic tradition

**Linguistic characteristics**:
- Highly inflected (7 cases, 3 numbers)
- Complex verbal system (5 conjugations, 4 past tenses)
- Rich aspect system
- Dual number preserved

#### [[OCS Legacy]]

**Cultural impact**:
- Enabled Slavic literacy
- Preserved early Slavic culture
- Facilitated conversion to Christianity

**Linguistic influence**:
- Basis for Church Slavonic
- Influence on all Slavic languages
- Source of literary vocabulary

### 15.2 From OCS to Modern Slavic Languages

#### [[Language Change]]

**Phonological changes**:
- Loss of jers (weak position)
- Simplification of consonant clusters
- Changes in vowel system
- Loss of pitch accent in many languages

**Morphological changes**:
- Loss of dual number (except Slovene, Sorbian)
- Simplification of case system (Bulgarian, Macedonian)
- Changes in verbal system
- New tense formations

**Syntactic changes**:
- More fixed word order
- Development of articles (Bulgarian, Macedonian)
- Changes in subordination patterns

#### [[Preservation vs. Innovation]]

**Conservative features** in some languages**:
- Slovene: Dual number, complex case system
- Sorbian: Dual number, rich morphology
- Russian: Church Slavonic influence

**Innovative features** in others**:
- Bulgarian: Loss of case, development of articles
- Macedonian: Loss of case, articles
- Russian: Loss of aorist/imperfect

### 15.3 Studying OCS Texts

#### [[Textual Analysis]]

**Steps for analysis**:
1. Identify word boundaries (scriptio continua)
2. Identify grammatical forms (case, number, gender, tense, etc.)
